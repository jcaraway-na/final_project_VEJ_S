Overview

Selected Topic: If the covid pandemic did not happen, how would traffic incidents compare to current traffic incidents since the pandemic began? Reason why we selected the topic: We selected this topic to analyze traffic trends throughout the Central Texas area. Description of the source data: Vision Zero Crash data is obtained from the Texas Department of Transportation (TXDOT) Crash Record Information System (CRIS) database, which is populated by reports submitted by Texas Peace Officers throughout the state, including Austin Police Department (APD), and maintained by TXDOT. Questions we hope to answer with the data: If the covid pandemic did not happen, what would traffic incidents look like through the current date? Since the covid pandemic happened, are commuters more or less safe/better or worse drivers?

The Random Forest model that had been trained on traffic incident data from 2013-2019 was not able to predict serious traffic incidents in 2020 at all. Especially at the very beginning of the pandemic, the actual serious accidents were far below what the model predicted for the majority of the year.

However, in 2021, the actual serious crashes were much closer to the predictions throughout the year.

Interestingly, crashes involving pedestrians continued to stay low in 2021 in contrast. After a dip in early 2020, the actual crashes involving pedestrians did not reach the model's prediction at any point in 2020 or 2021.

Looking at crashes by the day of the week and the hour shows that, while crashes were down during the COVID pandemic, traffic patterns remained the same. During COVID, crashes increased throughout the week, peaking on Fridays and Saturdays, and then decreasing again on Sunday -- which is the same pattern found in pre-COVID data, even though COVID crashes were much less overall. Similarly, evening rush hour (5-7 pm) was the most dangerous time to drive both before and during COVID, despite a decrease across the board in number of crashes.

The average number of all crashes per month in 2013-2019 far surpassed crashes in 2020 or 2021. You can see a sharp drop in March of 2020, and throughout the next two years as many are working remotely, crashes remain lower despite an increase in Austin's population. This, coupled with the fact that the patterns of crashes remained the same, suggests that the best way to decrease crashes is to simply have fewer cars on the road. COVID did not affect when people crash, but just decreased their likelihood by keeping former commuters at home. The only question that remains is why, if crashes overall are still down, and crashes involving pedestrians are down, why are serious crashes back up to pre-pandemic levels? It could be the result of reckless driving caused by stress, or that people who are primarily working from home are worse at driving than before.

Even controlling for the population of Austin, which continued to grow during the COVID pandemic, crashes were much lower during 2020. In the graph of accidents per capita per month, there's a precipitous drop in early 2020, when, despite more people living in Austin, fewer people were crashing in Austin.

Summary of Findings

The Random Forest Model's failure, along with other data analysis on crashes in the two time periods, show a huge decrease in traffic incidents. Because the overall pattern of crashes did not change, and because crashes decreased despite population growth, the most likely explanation is that the increase in remote work during the COVID pandemic simply reduced the number of people on the roads -- and thus the number of people hurt on the roads.
